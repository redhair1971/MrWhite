In this activity you will have the opportunity to review some chemistry from previous science courses and apply that knowledge to biological molecules.  Knowledge of chemistry prepares us for biochemistry and understanding the processes that occur within the cell at a molecular level. 

Let’s get started!

Review of Chemistry



Periodic table of Elements

From the above table we will focus on non-metals such as carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, sulphur and some metals such as sodium, potassium, calcium and magnesium.  You must be familiar with the names, symbols and bonding capacity of these elements.  The bonding capacity is the number of bonds that they can make.  For instance, carbon (C) has four electrons in its outermost shell.  To make one covalent (this type of bond is based on sharing electrons) bond, C can share an electron with another atom.  When two atoms share two electrons between them this produces a single bond.  When they share four electrons, it is a double bond and when they share six electrons it produces a triple bond.


Methane (CH4)

In this methane molecule, carbon is sharing four electrons with hydrogen. The diagram shows the outer most energy level of each atom overlapping and sharing two electrons to make one single covalent bond.

In biology, the main types of chemical bonds that exist in living systems are covalent bonds.   On the left, C shares four electrons with four H atoms.  The sharing of two electrons between C and H forms a single bond.  In the diagram on the right, C makes four single bonds with four H atoms.  C has a bonding capacity of four.  The molecule shown in both diagrams is methane (CH4).

Each pair of shared electrons makes a single bond. Carbon can make four single bonds and has a bonding capacity of four. When lines represent the shared electrons, the image created is called a structural diagram.

Electrons in the last energy level can be represented as dots beside the chemical symbol of the element.  This is called the electron dot diagram or Lewis Structure.  These structures show the shared electrons as well as the other electrons in the last energy level.  The shared electrons can also be replaced by lines as shown in the last column.  In ethylene, two carbon atoms share four electrons, creating a double bond. In acetylene 2 carbon atoms share 6 electrons, creating a triple bond.
